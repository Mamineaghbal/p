Sequence and Functions

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers following a specific pattern or rule. In calculus, sequences are fundamental for understanding limits and convergence.

A function is a relation that uniquely associates members of one set with members of another set. Functions are the building blocks of calculus.

Key Concepts:

- Convergence and divergence of sequences

- Limits of functions

- Continuity and differentiability

- Intermediate Value Theorem

Integration

Integration is one of the two main operations of calculus, with its inverse operation being differentiation. The definite integral represents the area under a curve.

Integration techniques include substitution, integration by parts, partial fractions, and trigonometric substitution.

Key Concepts:

- Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

- Riemann sums and definite integrals

- Applications: area, volume, arc length

- Improper integrals

Taylor Series

A Taylor series is a representation of a function as an infinite sum of terms calculated from the values of its derivatives at a single point.

The Taylor series of a real or complex-valued function f(x) that is infinitely differentiable at a real or complex number a is the power series:

Key Concepts:

- Maclaurin series (Taylor series centered at 0)

- Radius and interval of convergence

- Applications in approximations and physics

- Analytic functions

Series and Series of Function

A series is the sum of the terms of an infinite sequence. Series of functions involve summing functions rather than numbers.

Important types include power series, Fourier series, and Laurent series. Convergence of series of functions is more complex than numerical series.

Key Concepts:

- Pointwise and uniform convergence

- Weierstrass M-test

- Term-by-term differentiation and integration

- Power series representation

Back
Multivariable Function

Multivariable calculus extends calculus to functions of multiple variables. It includes partial derivatives, multiple integrals, and vector calculus.

Applications include optimization, physics, engineering, and economics where multiple variables interact.

Key Concepts:

- Partial derivatives and gradients

- Multiple integrals (double, triple)

- Vector fields and line integrals

- Green's, Stokes', and Divergence theorems

Back
Logic and Sets Theory

Mathematical logic is the study of formal logic within mathematics. Set theory is the branch of mathematical logic that studies sets.

Sets are collections of objects, and set theory provides a foundation for nearly all of mathematics.

Key Concepts:

- Propositional and predicate logic

- Set operations: union, intersection, complement

- Relations and functions

- Cardinality and countability

Back
Algebraic Structure

An algebraic structure consists of a nonempty set with one or more operations defined on it that satisfy certain axioms.

Common structures include groups, rings, fields, and vector spaces.

Key Concepts:

- Groups: closure, associativity, identity, inverse

- Rings and fields

- Homomorphisms and isomorphisms

- Lattices and Boolean algebras

Back
Vectorial Space and Matrices

A vector space is a set of vectors that can be added together and multiplied by scalars. Matrices are rectangular arrays of numbers used to represent linear transformations.

Linear algebra is fundamental to many areas of mathematics and its applications.

Key Concepts:

- Basis and dimension

- Linear transformations and matrices

- Eigenvalues and eigenvectors

- Orthogonality and projections

Back
Statistics and Aleatory Variables

Statistics involves collecting, analyzing, interpreting, and presenting data. Aleatory (random) variables represent numerical outcomes of random phenomena.

Probability theory provides the mathematical foundation for statistics.

Key Concepts:

- Probability distributions (discrete and continuous)

- Expected value and variance

- Central Limit Theorem

- Hypothesis testing and confidence intervals

Back
Introduction to Topology

Topology is concerned with the properties of a geometric object that are preserved under continuous deformations, such as stretching, twisting, and bending.

It provides a framework for understanding continuity, connectedness, and compactness.

Key Concepts:

- Topological spaces and open sets

- Continuity in topological spaces

- Connectedness and path-connectedness

- Compactness and Hausdorff spaces
